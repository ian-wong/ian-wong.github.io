IguanaChase

Scavenge. Discover. Survive.

Overview

Problem

As a team of 60 students, we were tasked to develop a 3D isometric survival game that involved crafting and exploration similar to games like Arc and Rust.

Background

Take the role of a frog stranded, lost and starving on the shores of mysterious islands where iguanas just really don't like you. You are the last of your kind living in freedom, the rest have been killed or captured. The king iguanas have an insatiable appetite for frogâ€™s legs and won't stop until they have been fed. The key to survival is hunting, gathering resources, crafting items and discovering new territories to build shelters that will withstand all the elements.

Audience

This game is targeted towards people who are 10+ years old who enjoys a crafting, survival themed game.

Role

My team focused on the items and crafting aspect of the game and I was assigned as the UI/UX designer and graphics designer where I designed a number of icons, items and conducted user testing for all the designs and the features that my team programmed.

Items

I was tasked to create the designs of each item for the player to pick up and use to craft more items. I collaborated with the entity teams closely to see what items that are wanted from the objects (e.g. trees, rocks) that can be destroyed. The team also collaborated with the crafting team to see which items are needed to be used to combine items to craft.

Character Icons

The character statistics team did not have a dedicated designer so, I offered to help design the icons for the character's statistics. The icons that I designed were the stamina and hunger which were represented by a green energy dots and drumsticks respectively. The statistics team wanted the stamina to look similar to the Zelda energy bar and Minecraft's hunger icon with the drumstick.

Inventory Interface

This is the inventory interface for the character where the player can get access to the items that are picked up and can be used to craft new items. The character can also wear armour and defend themselves with swords and shields. The level of the character and more detailed statistics can also be seen in the inventory interface. Due to time constraint the team did not get to implement a design for the inventory interface which means that this design did not get to be used into the real game.

Design Process

Research

Before starting the design and layout of the interface, I decided to research other inventory interfaces from different games to see what components are available to the user and why each component is important. The games that were chosen as research were Arc, Conan Exiles and Rust because of the similarities in the genre and the aspect of being able to freely explore to craft and survive.

The similarities between these three interfaces are that all consists areas for the inventory storage, crafting menu, an image of the actual character and slots for items that the character can equip. By identifying some common features, it helped define what the basic functionailities of a inventory interface should include and the importance of understanding how each feature fit together to hep the user have an easy time managing their equipment.

To gather more research about the inventory interface in games, I conducted a user testing session with participants who were young adults aged 19 - 22 who were avid gamers, familiar with open world survival games like Rust. I conducted the session using a survey asking the participants what are the most important features of the interface and to compare each of the three interfaces above.

I interviewed around 5 people and found that:

  • A simplistic interface design like Arc's inventory was the best out of the three because of where each element is positioned spaciously.
  • Including the most significant character statistics is important to understand the character's abilities and the current level of your character.
  • Implementing too many statistics and features will crowd the interface making the content difficult to see and comprehend.

Wireframe

From the feedback gained from the user testing session, I created two different wireframes that consist of the similiar elements in the interface but positioned differently. The first wireframe was designed to be a small pop up inventory interface like Minecraft's interface compared to the other wireframe which is a full screen interface.

Design Iteration

Aspects from the second wireframe was implemented to the mockup designs as users were happy with the size of the inventory and crafting area. Through iteration, the final mockup included some statistics about the character below the inventory and shows the item hotbar at the bottom to easily allow users to put items to and from the inventory.

Additionally, creating a button to craft items made it easier for the user to understand how to craft the item. I changed the colour scheme from gray as users stated that it was bland and boring to have a dull background colour.

Final Outcome

In summary, the final outcome for the team was a success in terms of designing and developing the items and the interaction with the player and the items. However, the inventory interface did not get implemented which created an unappealing design and experience from the rest of the game. I was proud of the work that I contributed to the project as a whole since working together as a team of 60 students was definitely one of the biggest challenges that I faced. Making sure that everyone understood what needs to be done each week to keep aligned with the project's requirements took a lot of meetings and stand ups but after a couple weeks, the communication between teams were brilliant.
