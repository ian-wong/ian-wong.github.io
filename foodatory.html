Foodatory
A tutorial mode for cooking!

Overview

Problem

The overall theme for the studio is “Designing for playful and open ended interactions in everyday life” where specifically for Foodatory is “Learning Through Play”. Foodatory aims to be a solution to two issues. First, it aims to provide its users with a novel experience that will spark an interest in cooking in the user themselves. Secondly, Foodatory reinforces core concepts of food safety by providing the user with guidance should they do something 'unsafe'.

Background

Foodatory is a collection of highly interactive mini-games designed to introduce basic cooking skills and the concept of food safety in a fun, safe and educational way. These games involve some of the core physical motions used in cooking such as stirring, spreading and cutting to create a gigantic pizza. The games revolve around a selection of 'smart' kitchen utensils, each of which are capable of providing a variety of feedback to the user in order to help them smarter and safer decisions.

Audience

It is targeted towards a younger audience - primarily children aged from 5 to 8 years old as they are constantly exposed to cooking but may still not understand the concepts of food safety and cleanliness. It will be a one-off incursion event where Foodatory will be presented to different schools as an extra afternoon activity for the children to enjoy during school hours.

Role

Programmer, UX Designer, Resource Manager

My main contribution consisted of the features and implementations for the cutting knife, however I did put effort into helping everyone and get involved in each part of the project. I was also in charge of researching and buying all our materials and resources needed.

Interaction Process
  • Beginning

    Foodatory begins with the users going to the sink and washing their hands which teaches their the importance of hygiene and cleaniness.

  • Roll the Dough

    After washing their hands, the users will work together to roll out the 'dough' of the pizza.

  • Split into different stations

    Followed by another round of washing their hands again, the users will be then divided into two sub stations - the cutting and stirring station.

  • Cutting Station

    The cutting station will focus on cutting up all the topping ingredients. The available ingredients to cut are salami, mushroom, tomato, pineapple, cheese and olives.

  • Stirring Station

    The stirring station will focus on creating the sauce for the pizza with the 3 choices - Pizza sauce, BBQ sauce or Cheese sauce. After selecting the sauce, the user will have to stir the sauce ingredients together.

  • Bring it together

    When the sauce is ready, the stirring team will spread the sauce over the dough using the ladle, followed by the cutting team laying out the toppings

  • Photo finish

    In the end, the whole team will take a group photo with their pizza creation.

Knife Technology

To bring awareness on the dangers of kitchen utensil tools when the user touches the blade of the knife, it will trigger three effects. An alert sound will go off, vibration will shake the knife and lights will go off.

Capacitive Touch

When a person touches the end of the blade, it triggers the rest of the technology.

Haptic Vibration

An haptic vibration is connected to the handle so vibration could be felt through the entire knife.

Neopixel

A strip of Neopixels is put on top of the knife and when triggered a red light will display.

DFPlayer Mini

An MP3 file of a alert sound was stored in the SD card.

A speaker was also connected so when it was triggered the sound could be played.

Ladle & Bowl Technology

Neopixel

Around the bowl, the Neopixel will progressively light up depending on the amount of stirring motion has been done and light up green when it is complete.

Accelerometer

An accelerometer is used for the ladle to detect any stirring movement is made so the Neopixels could light up.

RFID

An RFID reader and tags are used to determine which sauce is being put on the pizza. A number of sauce ingredients will be selected and tapped to recognise the pizza sauce.

Foodatory Setup

Design Process

An iterative, incremental development process similar to Agile was implemented throughout the development of Foodatory. Since this project had no clear requirements at the beginning, enforcing an iterative process that can adapt to rapidly changing requirements is key because frequent cycles allowed the team to check and refine against the user's expectations of the experience. Each design cycle had 4 key phases: Research, Analyse, Design, Prototype, Test.

1st Iteration

Intially, we wanted to focus on the technical aspects of cooking where practicing knife skills, fine motor skills and hand-eye coordination using high technological kitchen tools were used. We researched into the background of understanding why many young adults did not know how to cook and survive into adulthood without any culinary skills. The one major concern we had was that it did not create a playful interactions rather it was a boring training mechanism which did not fit the studio theme of designing playful, open-ended interactions.

After pitching the concept to the class and we received valuable feedback that our concept should focus on the novelty aspect of cooking and target a much younger audience to enhance the joyful experience. From there we decided to change our concept focusing on the novelty and fun aspect to the game and removed the technical aspect.

2nd Iteration

Following on from changing the concept, the team shifted the research to focus on healthy eating and cooking for children and how relying on convenient meals can have a huge impact on their health. Subsequently, the team focused on the interactions at each different station and how the concept can be organised together into a fun experience. Vertical prototyping was used to create a basic prototype for all the interactions so that we could have a sense of how the concept would be interacted. I focused on the knife where I separately coded the vibration, sound, lights and touch sensor first, before joining everything together. The initial prototype of the form of the knife was created out of cardboard to get sense of the size and motion of cutting.

3rd Iteration

The final major iteration of the developmental process was the physical building of each ingredient, kitchen tool and scoping down some of the ideas due to time constraints. The knife had been enlarged and made out of moldable poly-doh to create it out of a stronger material. The spoon of the ladle was also created out of poly-doh. Due to time constraints, a sink basin could not be created so the physical construction was ruled out.

Final Outcome

Overall, Foodatory's final outcome was a huge success when it was presented in the exhibit at The Edge. We were surprised by the amount of users who enjoyed it, as both aspect of novelty and food safety were aimed at a much younger audience so we initially thought that it would not be as successful. During the exhibit, the team introduced Foodatory and explained each different station to create a fun, collaborative experience. To simulate the hand washing interaction, the team used a gel hand sanitiser instead as a sink could not be retrieved for the exhibit. Also, there was a number of occassions where there were issues with the capacitive touch sensor in the dough. The sensor was not able to detech the capacitive touch through the ladle which led to the sauce not projecting onto the pizza. To counteract the issue, we faked the dough technology to a certain degree, by touching the capacitive touch pins directly when the user spread the sauce.
